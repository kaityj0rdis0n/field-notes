# State nexus

## TL;DR

State nexus = a sufficient legal connection between a business and a US state that lets the state tax the business, or require it to collect taxes on the state's behalf. Two flavors: **physical** and **economic**.

## The explanation

### Physical nexus

The traditional rule. You have physical nexus in a state if you have:

- Offices, warehouses, or servers there.
- Employees or contractors there.
- Inventory stored there (this catches Amazon FBA sellers a lot).
- Even temporary presence — traveling salespeople, trade shows beyond a threshold.

If you have physical nexus, the state can tax you (income tax, sales tax collection duty, franchise tax, gross receipts tax, etc.).

### Economic nexus

The newer rule, established by [*South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc.*, 138 S. Ct. 2080 (2018)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/585/17-494/). Before *Wayfair*, you needed physical presence in a state for the state to require sales tax collection. *Wayfair* overturned that — a state can now require collection based purely on economic activity: exceeding a sales-dollar threshold or transaction-count threshold within the state.

Common state thresholds (varies by state):
- $100,000 in sales **OR** 200 transactions per year. Some states are just dollar-based now.
- Measured on a rolling 12-month or calendar-year basis.

### Marketplace facilitator laws

A follow-on consequence of *Wayfair*: most states passed laws making **platforms** responsible for collecting sales tax on behalf of their third-party sellers, once the platform itself exceeds the state threshold. So a small Etsy seller doesn't have to register in 50 states — Etsy does it for them.

### What nexus triggers

| Type of nexus | What it can trigger |
|---|---|
| Physical | State income tax, sales tax, franchise tax, gross receipts tax, payroll tax |
| Economic | Sales tax collection (post-*Wayfair*); income tax in most states |
| Marketplace facilitator | Sales tax collection duty on behalf of platform sellers |

### Apportionment

Once you have income-tax nexus in multiple states, you don't pay 100% income tax in each — you apportion your income across them. Historically the rule was a three-factor formula (property, payroll, sales). Most states have moved to a single-sales-factor formula. For service revenue, "market-based sourcing" (revenue sourced to where the customer benefits) now dominates over the older "cost-of-performance" method.

## When does this matter?

- **E-commerce / SaaS expanding into new states**: each state you cross a threshold in adds compliance burden.
- **Remote work**: a single employee living in a new state can create physical nexus there for the employer.
- **Marketplaces / platforms**: trigger collection duties in nearly every state.
- **M&A diligence**: an acquirer will scrutinize the target's unfiled state tax exposure.
- **Inventory stored in fulfillment networks**: physical nexus you may not even know you have.
