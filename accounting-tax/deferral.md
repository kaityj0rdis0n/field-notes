# Deferral

## TL;DR

Deferral = pushing recognition of revenue (or an expense) into a later accounting period than when the cash actually changed hands. It's the bridge between "we got paid" and "we earned it."

## The explanation

Cash and revenue aren't the same thing in accrual accounting.

Example — a customer pays $100 today for a service you'll deliver next year:

- Cash hits your bank account today → it's in your bank account, no question.
- But the **service** hasn't happened yet → you haven't really "earned" the money. You owe them a service.
- So instead of booking $100 of revenue today, you record it as a **liability** on the balance sheet: "deferred revenue" or "unearned revenue."
- When you actually deliver the service, you move it off the liability line and onto the income statement as revenue. That's when it's "recognized."

Quick analogy: deferral is the accounting equivalent of "I have your money, but I haven't done the thing yet, so I can't count it as earnings."

The same logic runs in reverse for expenses (deferred expenses / prepaid expenses) — you've paid the cash but haven't consumed the value yet, so it sits as an asset until you use it up.

## When does this matter?

- **Subscription businesses**: someone pays for a year up front. The provider can't recognize the whole year of revenue on day 1 — it gets spread over the 12 months.
- **Ticketing**: tickets sold months before the event sit in deferred revenue until the event happens.
- **Prepayments / retainers**: a client pays a retainer for legal work. Revenue is recognized as the lawyer actually does the work.
- **Year-end planning**: which side of December 31 the recognition falls on can change which tax year the income shows up in.
