# Revenue recognition (accrual basis)

## TL;DR

Under accrual accounting, you recognize revenue when you've **earned** it — i.e., delivered the good or service — not when the cash hits. For tax purposes, this is governed by the IRS "all-events test."

## The explanation

Two main accounting methods:

- **Cash method**: revenue recognized when cash is received. Expenses recognized when paid. Simple. Used by small businesses and some service businesses.
- **Accrual method**: revenue recognized when earned (regardless of when cash arrives). Expenses recognized when incurred (regardless of when paid). Required for most larger businesses, especially those with inventory.

### The IRS "all-events test"

For accrual-method taxpayers, the IRS rule (under [IRC § 451(a)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/451) and [Treas. Reg. § 1.451-1(a)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.451-1)) is that revenue is recognized when:

1. The right to receive the income is **fixed** (i.e., the customer can't take it back without cause), AND
2. The amount can be **reasonably determined**.

In plain English: revenue is "earned" when the performance obligation is satisfied. For a service, that's when the service has been delivered. For a product, that's typically when title passes.

### Advance payments — the one-year deferral rule

If you take payment up front for something you'll deliver later, you generally don't have to recognize it as income immediately. Under [IRC § 451(c)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/451) and the regulations at [Treas. Reg. § 1.451-8](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.451-8), accrual-method taxpayers can defer recognition of an advance payment for **one tax year**.

Worked example:
- Cash received in 2026 for a service delivered in 2026 → recognize in 2026.
- Cash received in December 2026 for a service delivered in March 2027 → can be deferred to 2027.
- Cash received in 2026 for a service delivered in 2028 → must be recognized by 2027 at the latest. The IRS won't let you defer past one year, even if the service is further out.

This rule was codified into § 451(c) by the **TCJA (Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017)**, [P.L. 115-97](https://www.congress.gov/bill/115th-congress/house-bill/1).

### Tax vs. GAAP

For book purposes (GAAP), revenue recognition follows [ASC 606](https://asc.fasb.org/) — recognize revenue as performance obligations are satisfied. The IRS generally now requires tax treatment to follow book treatment under the "AFS (Applicable Financial Statement) conformity rule" at IRC § 451(b), unless a permitted special method applies.

In other words: tax and GAAP have converged on the same answer for most situations. The IRS just adds the one-year cap on deferral that GAAP doesn't have.

## When does this matter?

- **Ticketing / event businesses**: ticket sold today, event next year → revenue recognized at the event, parked in deferred revenue until then.
- **SaaS / subscriptions**: annual prepayment → revenue spread over the year.
- **Construction / long-term projects**: there's a whole separate set of methods (percentage-of-completion, completed contract).
- **Year-end planning**: small shifts in delivery date can move revenue between tax years.
- **Cancellations**: when a deal is cancelled before delivery, the deferred revenue reverses and (depending on retention terms) some may become recognizable as breakage.
