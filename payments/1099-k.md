# 1099-K reporting

## TL;DR

The 1099-K is the IRS information return that **payment platforms** (Payment Settlement Entities — PSEs) file to report payments processed on behalf of third-party sellers. It's *not* the same as a 1099-NEC (paid contractors) or a W-2 (paid employees) — it's a payment-flow report.

## The explanation

### Who files

Any "Payment Settlement Entity" (PSE) — a company that processes payment-card or third-party-network transactions on behalf of merchants/sellers. This includes:

- Payment processors (Stripe, Square, PayPal).
- Marketplaces and platforms (eBay, Etsy, ticketing platforms, ridesharing apps).
- Card networks.

The legal basis is [IRC § 6050W](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/6050W) and [Treas. Reg. § 1.6050W-1](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.6050W-1).

### What it reports

Gross payment volume to each merchant/seller, by month and total for the year. Not net of fees, not net of refunds — gross. (The merchant reconciles fees/refunds separately on their own tax return.)

### The threshold history (this has been moving)

Historically: $20,000 in gross payments **AND** 200 transactions per year. Quite high — most casual sellers never got one.

In 2021, the **American Rescue Plan Act** ([P.L. 117-2 § 9674](https://www.congress.gov/bill/117th-congress/house-bill/1319/text)) lowered the threshold to **$600** (with no transaction-count floor). This was a huge change — suddenly every casual reseller, gig worker, and side-hustler was in scope.

The IRS has phased this in via guidance to avoid a tsunami of forms:

- [Notice 2023-10](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-23-10.pdf) — delayed for tax year 2022.
- [Notice 2023-74](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-23-74.pdf) — delayed again for 2023.
- [Notice 2024-85](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-24-85.pdf) — phased schedule:
  - **$5,000** for tax year 2024
  - **$2,500** for tax year 2025
  - **$600** for tax year 2026 and beyond

(Newer legislation could shift this — verify against current IRS guidance for any specific year.)

### What sellers need to do

Sellers receiving a 1099-K need a Taxpayer Identification Number on file with the platform — usually collected via a **W-9** (for US persons) or **W-8BEN** (for foreign persons). Platforms can't file a 1099-K without recipient info.

The 1099-K reports gross payment volume to the IRS *and* to the seller, so when the seller files their own taxes, the IRS already has the volume number. They need to reconcile fees, refunds, COGS, etc. themselves to arrive at taxable income.

### State-level overlay

Several states have their own 1099-K thresholds, often lower than the federal one (e.g., Massachusetts and Vermont had $600 thresholds before the federal change). Platforms have to track state-by-state thresholds in addition to federal.

## When does this matter?

- **Building a payments platform / marketplace**: you'll need a W-9 collection flow and 1099-K issuance/delivery system.
- **Selling on a platform**: above the threshold, you'll get one; below, you may not. Either way, the income is still taxable — the 1099-K just makes it visible to the IRS.
- **Stripe Connect-backed platforms**: Stripe issues 1099-Ks for some Connect account types directly to connected accounts, so the *platform's* filing obligation depends on the Connect configuration (Standard / Express / Custom) and who controls pricing.
